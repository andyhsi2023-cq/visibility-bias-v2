Dear Professor Mattingly,

I am writing to share a measurement project on Chinese municipal cadre attention and to ask for your reaction, given your work on informal political control and local governance in China.

Your book *The Art of Political Control in China* motivated much of my thinking about how local officials' observable behavior relates to unobservable incentives. Where your work exploits variation in mayor/secretary characteristics to identify governance patterns, I have taken a text-based route — constructing a Visibility Attention Index (VAI) from 6,294 prefecture-level government work reports (2002–2024) that operationalizes a cadre-attention model with observability asymmetry.

The paper's main methodological contribution is a **within-document retrospective-vs-prospective differential**: the past-year review sections of GWRs are systematically more visibility-loaded than the prospective plan sections (Δ = +0.025, paired t = 8.4, p < 10⁻¹⁶, N = 4,330). This is a behavioral test of compositional attention that is identified purely from *within* a single author's single document — impervious to cross-city or cross-author unobservables.

The paper is pre-registered at OSF (DOI 10.17605/OSF.IO/ZMJY5) and I report two pre-registered null results transparently. Most notably, an event study around central anti-corruption inspections *does not* survive heterogeneity-robust estimation when the sample is extended to 2017 — which I report as a definitive null (sign reverses from narrow-window TWFE −0.065 to expanded-window Sun-Abraham +0.016). I think this kind of honest null-reporting is what the field needs more of, and I would value your view on whether the way I handle this in §5.4.1 is credible.

I would welcome your engagement at whatever level works for your schedule — substantive feedback, acknowledgment, or co-authorship. I am finalizing the manuscript (~9,500 words) and am considering methodology-focused venues (Political Analysis; Research & Politics; Journal of Computational Social Science).
