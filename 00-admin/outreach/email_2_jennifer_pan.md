Dear Professor Pan,

I am writing to share a text-measurement project on Chinese municipal government work reports and to ask for your reaction, given how directly it touches on themes in your work on computational approaches to the Chinese state.

Your work on the 50 Cent Army (AJPS) and in *Welcome to the Party* established what careful text-based measurement of Chinese political communication looks like — including the importance of pre-registration, construct validity, and explicit scope claims. I have tried to apply the same discipline to a different measurement target: the compositional share of *visible* vs. *functional* policy attention in 6,294 annual government work reports (282 prefectures, 2002–2024).

The instrument — which I call the Visibility Attention Index (VAI) — operationalizes a principal-agent model in which a supervisor observes both categories with asymmetric noise (observable boulevards, unobservable sewers), and the official's rhetorical composition reveals the resulting career-concern wedge. The paper is pre-registered at OSF (DOI 10.17605/OSF.IO/ZMJY5) and passes four of five pre-registered construct-validity tests.

The key methodological move is a **within-document retrospective-vs-prospective differential**: past-year review sections are systematically more visibility-loaded than next-year plan sections (Δ = +0.025, paired t = 8.4, p < 10⁻¹⁶, N = 4,330 GWRs). Same lexicon, same author, same report — so the differential is not confounded by any of the usual suspects. To my knowledge, this kind of within-document behavioral signature is novel in the text-as-data literature.

The fifth construct-validity test — a pre-registered external validation against Chinese Wikipedia city descriptions — returned a null (r = −0.15), which I interpret as a scope finding rather than as evidence of invalid measurement. I would particularly value your view on whether my domain-scope framing (governance-rhetoric, full stop) is defensible.

I would value your reaction at whatever level works for your schedule — substantive feedback only, acknowledgment if comments materially improve the paper, or co-authorship if you find the measurement contribution worth developing further.
