Dear Professor Jiang,

I am writing to share a text-measurement instrument I have been developing for Chinese municipal government work reports and to ask whether it might be of interest for your work on cadre incentives and bureaucratic performance.

Your 2018 AJPS paper, *Making Bureaucracy Work*, has been foundational to my thinking about how to measure cadre attention beyond outcome-based measures. Where your paper uses patronage networks to identify variation in bureaucratic effort, I have taken a complementary route — constructing a **Visibility Attention Index (VAI)** from the textual share of visible vs. functional urban-renewal discourse in 6,294 annual government work reports across 282 prefectures (2002–2024). The core idea is that when a supervisor's observability is asymmetric across categories, the official's rhetorical composition reveals the career-concern wedge directly.

Beyond the aggregate VAI, the paper's novel methodological contribution is a **within-document retrospective-vs-prospective differential**: the past-year review section of each GWR is systematically more visibility-loaded than the next-year plan section (Δ = +0.025, paired t = 8.4, p < 10⁻¹⁶, N = 4,330). The test uses the same lexicon, same author, and same report, and cannot be produced by any of the standard text-measurement confounds. I believe this is a new kind of within-document behavioral signature for compositional policy attention, not previously available in the text-as-data literature.

The paper is pre-registered at OSF (DOI 10.17605/OSF.IO/ZMJY5), with a full five-test construct-validity battery. I report two pre-registered null results transparently, including one — an event study around central anti-corruption inspections — that does not survive heterogeneity-robust estimation under an extended sample (which I report as a definitive null rather than bury).
